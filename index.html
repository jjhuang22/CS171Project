chapter 1: the birth of a startup


Once a founder has an idea, one of the first big decisions they make is where to base their company. Location influences the talent, investors, market, mentorship, and resources a budding startup has access to. Many founders choose to begin their journey in highly concentrated urban areas like Silicon Valley and New York City, where they can take advantage of network effects.


But because of that, competition can be fierce.

chapter 2: baby steps


The world of startups is notoriously cutthroat. To survive and grow, a startup needs capital. Where does it come from?


First, let’s clear up some vocab. There are all kinds of buzzwords surrounding startup funding: Series A/B/C, seed funding, angel investors, venture capital, crowdfunding... (have these float in) that it can be hard to decipher what it all means.


The funding progression looks different for every startup, but it is common for startups to pursue venture capital to grow their business. The ones that don't may rely on crowdfunding or “bootstrapping” (self-funding and borrowing money from family and friends).


Let's focus on venture funding. The earliest stage often consists of seed funding or angel investors, whose money goes toward building the product and proving that the idea is worth pursuing.

Then, Series A funding (which often lasts for 18-24 months) helps a company scale its operations and minimize risk.

Finally, Series B and C funding is when the company expands, utilizing the growth machinery that it created during Series A. By the time a company reaches Series B, it is already well-established; by the time it reaches Series C, it can be termed quite successful.

chapter 3: journey's end?


What is success?


Generally, venture-funded startups have two exit options once they mature: go public or sell.


When a company goes public, or IPOs, it makes a splash in the business world. (have headlines zoom in on parallax in the background) From 2007 to 2013, the average IPO-bound startup raised $162 million in funding for a $467.9 million IPO. Highlight Facebook and Twitter as major IPOs in 2012/2013

Getting acquired is less flashy but fills pockets nonetheless: the average startup that is successfully acquired raised $29.4 million and sold for $155.5 million. Examples include YouTube

The trend is clear: there is a strong positive relationship between the amount of money a company raises and the amount that they exit for. Acquisition takes slightly less time than an IPO (on average, 9 months fewer).
